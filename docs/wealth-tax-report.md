# Taxing wealth: what persuades — and what £24bn a year could do

*Research note for the UK Wealth Dashboard · July 2026. Sourced throughout; figures are published estimates, not the dashboard's own modelling.*

---

## TL;DR

- The arguments for taxing wealth that survive contact with the *other* side's values are: **work is over-taxed relative to wealth** (a tax swap, not a tax rise), **fair competition** (an entrepreneur shouldn't face a higher effective rate than an heir), and **unavoidable bases** (land, property, and one-off retrospective levies that can't emigrate).
- The evidence on "the rich will leave" is more modest than the headlines: exit is rare, avoidance is common, and design determines which one you get.
- A 2% tax on assets over £10m — about 20,000 people, 0.04% of the UK — is estimated to raise **~£24bn a year** (Tax Justice UK). The strongest-evidenced uses for the most disadvantaged are: **cash to the poorest families** (fastest poverty reduction per pound), **Sure Start-style early years** (best long-run UK evidence), and **social housing at scale** (pays for itself over 30 years).

---

## Part 1 — The arguments that work across the spectrum

The strongest case for any tax is one framed in the values of the person you're persuading. Three framings recur across right, centre and left.

### From the right: a tax swap, not a tax rise

- **Work and enterprise are over-taxed; wealth sits under-taxed.** Someone on £35,000 loses roughly a third of their income to tax; [LSE/Warwick analysis of HMRC data](https://www.lse.ac.uk/research/research-for-the-world/economics/how-much-tax-do-the-rich-really-pay) (Advani & Summers) found people with £10m in total remuneration paid an average effective rate of **21%**, and a quarter of those receiving over £3m paid **12% or less**. A wealth tax pitched as *funding cuts to income tax, corporation tax or VAT* is a supply-side argument, not a redistribution argument.
- **Fair competition.** Pro-market conservatives object to a system where returns to lobbying, land banking and inheritance outcompete returns to building businesses. [UnHerd's "The Right-wing case for wealth tax"](https://unherd.com/2026/06/the-right-wing-case-for-wealth-tax/) argues the current settlement undermines the prosperous middle class, that revenue could fund law-and-order priorities, and that **inheritance tax could be abolished in its stead** — a straight trade many on the right would take.
- **Fiscal conservatism.** Deficit reduction without further squeezing middle earners.
- **The voters are already there.** [YouGov finds ~three-quarters of Britons support wealth taxes on the very rich](https://yougov.co.uk/politics/articles/45044-three-quarters-britons-support-wealth-taxes-millio), including **61% of Conservative voters**; [Best for Britain polling found 61% of Reform-considering voters](https://www.bestforbritain.org/decoding_populism_wealth_taxes) back a one-off 1% tax on wealth over £10m. [65% of UK millionaires themselves](https://patrioticmillionaires.uk/latest-news/policy-recommendations-2025) support a 2% tax over £10m (Patriotic Millionaires UK). In [July 2025 YouGov polling](https://yougov.co.uk/politics/articles/53468-can-the-uks-financial-problems-be-fixed-solely-by-taxing-the-rich), only 13% of voters opposed a 2% tax over £10m.

### From the centre and the economics profession: tax the unavoidable, undistorting bases

- **Land value tax** is the closest thing economics has to a consensus tax. Milton Friedman called it the ["least bad tax"](https://moneyweek.com/497288/land-value-tax-the-least-bad-tax) because land's supply is fixed — taxing it creates no deadweight loss. Support runs from [Adam Smith and Henry George to Friedman and Stiglitz](https://thelandcollective.com/land-value-tax/); in 1990 [four Nobel laureates (Modigliani, Solow, Tobin, Vickrey) signed an open letter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Land_value_tax) advocating land value taxation; Churchill campaigned for it. Crucially, **land cannot emigrate** — it is the archetypal unavoidable base.
- **The one-off levy.** The [Wealth Tax Commission](https://www.wealthandpolicy.com/wp/WealthTaxFinalReport.pdf) (Advani, Chamberlain & Summers, 2020) — the most thorough UK study — concluded a **one-off** wealth tax is economically efficient precisely because it's assessed on wealth at a *past* date: it cannot be avoided by changing behaviour, applies to recent emigrants and non-doms, and is payable in instalments over five years. At 1% a year for five years on millionaire couples it would raise **£260bn**. Its key insight: it's not redistribution per se, it's *the most progressive way to raise any given revenue*.
- **Horizontal equity.** A pound of income from wealth is taxed less than a pound earned from work (lower CGT rates, uplift at death, unrealised gains untaxed). Equalising treatment is a neutrality argument, not an egalitarian one.

### From the left: inequality itself is the harm

The moral case writes itself from the destitution data (Part 2), but the distinct left argument is about **power**: concentrated wealth buys political influence, and [JRF finds 3.8 million people experienced destitution in 2022, including a million children](https://www.jrf.org.uk/deep-poverty-and-destitution/destitution-in-the-uk-2023), while effective tax rates *fall* at the very top. The popularity data above means this is one of the rare left positions with majority support among every party's voters.

### Which taxes can't be dodged?

Ranked by unavoidability:

1. **Land and property** — immovable by definition (LVT, proportional property tax, council-tax revaluation).
2. **One-off retrospective levies** — assessed on past wealth; leaving afterwards doesn't help (Wealth Tax Commission design).
3. **Exit charges** — deemed disposal of assets on emigration (Canada and Australia already do this), which converts "I'll leave" into a tax event.
4. **Annual wealth taxes** — avoidable at the margins, which is why **design is everything**: high threshold, no exemptions, all asset classes.

### The flight question, honestly

- The academic literature is [close to settled that wealth taxes do not cause mass flight](https://www.forbes.com/sites/teresaghilarducci/2025/12/29/do-wealth-taxes-really-make-billionaires-leave/): **exit is rare; avoidance is common**. US millionaires move between states *less* than the general population (2.4% vs 2.9% a year) despite zero-income-tax states existing.
- **Norway is the honest test case**: after its 2022 increase, [some of the largest fortunes did leave](https://standrewseconomist.com/2026/06/18/the-norwegian-wealth-exodus/) — but [departures were far smaller than reported, revenue rose](https://en.ara.cat/economy/the-wealth-tax-in-norway-the-millionaires-do-not-flee-as-much-as-they-say_1_5718813.html), and researchers found [no evidence the tax restricted firm-level investment or employment](https://diarytimes.com/2025/11/24/norways-wealth-tax-sparks-millionaire-exodus-but-reinforces-its-equality-model/).
- The serious counter-cases deserve naming: the [IEA's "Fool's Gold"](https://iea.org.uk/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/IEA_Wealth-Tax_v3-Digital.pdf) and [Tax Policy Associates](https://taxpolicy.org.uk/2025/07/22/uk-wealth-tax-anti-growth/) argue an annual wealth tax is high-risk and anti-growth, and most European annual wealth taxes were repealed. But the repealed taxes shared the design flaws the modern proposals avoid: **low thresholds, riddled exemptions, and no exit charge**. The Wealth Tax Commission's answer — prefer a one-off levy; if annual, high threshold and no exemptions — already concedes the strongest critique.

## Part 2 — What ~£24bn a year should buy

The [Tax Justice UK / Patriotic Millionaires proposal](https://taxjustice.uk/campaign/taxing-wealth/): 2% on individual wealth above £10m ≈ **£24bn a year** from ~20,000 people. Treat the number as illustrative (static estimate; behavioural response would trim it). What follows is ranked by *strength of evidence of impact per pound on the most disadvantaged*.

### 1. Cash to the poorest families — fastest poverty reduction per pound

The best-evidenced lever in the UK toolkit. [CPAG costings](https://cpag.org.uk/news/new-costings-scrapping-two-child-limit-far-most-cost-effective-way-cut-child-poverty) showed scrapping the two-child limit was "by far" the most cost-effective anti-poverty policy ever costed — **£2bn to lift 350,000 children out of poverty instantly** and reduce poverty depth for 800,000 more. It was [abolished at the Autumn 2025 Budget](https://cpag.org.uk/news/abolition-two-child-limit-gamechanger-millions-children) — proof this lever gets pulled when the argument lands. The next rungs on the same ladder:

- **+£17/week on the UC child element ≈ £3bn** for a comparable poverty effect (CPAG).
- **The Essentials Guarantee** ([JRF/Trussell](https://www.jrf.org.uk/social-security/guarantee-our-essentials-reforming-universal-credit-to-ensure-we-can-all-afford-the)): peg UC's standard allowance to the actual cost of essentials (£120/week for a single adult vs the £98 it will pay from April 2026). [Nine in ten low-income UC households currently go without at least one essential](https://www.jrf.org.uk/news/universal-credit-falling-so-far-short-of-the-cost-of-essentials-is-putting-the-health-of). Cost: [~£20bn a year](https://national.thelead.uk/p/child-poverty-universal-credit-essentials-guarantee-cost-living-crisis) — almost exactly the wealth tax's yield. **One tax on 20,000 people ends crisis-level poverty for millions.**

The causal evidence that family income *itself* improves children's health, schooling and later earnings is [strong and international](https://www.nesta.org.uk/blog/welfare-reimagined-could-cash-transfers-combat-child-poverty/).

### 2. Sure Start-style early years — the best long-run UK evidence

The IFS evaluation of Sure Start is the closest thing Britain has to a proven social-mobility programme:

- Children near a centre gained **+0.8 GCSE grades on average; +3 grades for free-school-meals children** ([IFS](https://ifs.org.uk/news/sure-start-greatly-improved-disadvantaged-childrens-gcse-results)).
- At its peak it **prevented 13,000 hospitalisations a year** among 11–15-year-olds; NHS savings alone offset ~31% of its cost ([IFS](https://ifs.org.uk/news/their-peak-sure-start-centres-prevented-13000-hospitalisations-year-among-11-15-year-olds)).
- Benefits were [largest for low-income and ethnic-minority children](https://ifs.org.uk/news/sure-starts-wide-ranging-and-long-lasting-benefits-highlight-impact-integrated-early-years), and lifetime-earnings gains alone (~£1.09 per £1) roughly repay the outlay before counting health.

Rebuilding an integrated early-years service at Sure Start's peak intensity costs roughly **£2.5–3bn a year** — pocket change against £24bn, with the strongest long-horizon returns of anything on this list.

### 3. Social housing at scale — the investment that pays for itself

[CEBR modelling for Shelter and the National Housing Federation](https://england.shelter.org.uk/support_us/campaigns/social_housing/brick_by_brick_report_full): building **90,000 social homes a year** generates **£51.2bn in net benefits over 30 years and breaks even within 3**, via construction activity, £4.5bn saved on housing benefit, £4.5bn on temporary accommodation, and £5.2bn in NHS savings from healthier homes. At the dashboard's ~£140k grant per home, 90,000 homes ≈ **£12.6bn a year**. This is the option that most directly attacks the *wealth* side of inequality — it gives the poorest households the asset security the top decile takes for granted.

### Two honest packages (each ≈ £24bn/yr)

**A. End crisis poverty now:** Essentials Guarantee (~£20bn) + UC child element top-up (~£3bn). Maximum immediate effect on destitution, hunger and cold homes; the fastest measurable change in the lives of the worst-off.

**B. Floor + futures:** UC child element top-up (£3bn) + Sure Start rebuild (£3bn) + 90,000 social homes a year (£12.6bn) + a first step toward the Essentials Guarantee (~£5bn, ≈ £15/week on the standard allowance). Less immediate depth-of-poverty relief than A, but builds the assets — homes, early-childhood capability — that compound across generations.

The evidence points to a sequencing rule rather than a winner: **cash stops the bleeding now; early years and housing change the trajectory.** A serious anti-inequality programme does A-then-B or a blend, and either is affordable on the yield of one 2% tax on 0.04% of the population.

## Part 3 — What this means for the dashboard

- The dashboard's existing framing (your effective rate vs. theirs; "what could this pay for?") is exactly the cross-spectrum framing Part 1 says persuades — tax cuts first, services second.
- A natural extension: a third scenario in the tax-take section — "a 2% tax on wealth over £10m (~£24bn)" — alongside the two personalised scenarios, with pay-for cards drawn from the menu above (child poverty, Sure Start, social homes) — each with the source links in this report.
- Counterarguments belong on the page, not hidden: the Norway nuance and the one-off-vs-annual design point make the case *more* credible, not less.
